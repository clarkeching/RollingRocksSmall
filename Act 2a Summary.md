### Chapter 26: Cook to Demand

Cheryl takes them to the deep fryers.

*"Unspoken rule Number One: Don't run out of chips. Rule Two: Don't serve cold chips."*

The revelation: *"Our serving staff keeps an eye on how many chips they've got out front, and when it gets too low, they shout out and we cook more."*

Craig calls this **"replenish on demand."**

Cheryl switched to cooking vegetables in small batches. The first day was nuts. The second day was less nuts. Things kept getting better.

Now they prep vegetables before lunch rush, cook a first small batch just before noon, and servers shout when they need more. If it's busy, they cook larger batches based on demand.

Results: 30% more customers, 40% more revenue. They saved their jobs.

Steve: *"So cooking small batches not only improved your product quality, it also solved your forecasting problem."*

*"Exactly!"* says Cheryl.

### Chapter 31: Washing Up Epiphanies

Steve does the dishes while thinking through the clouds. Mom cooked mushroom risotto. He works through pros and cons of building many features vs. building only high-value features.

He realizes: There's a natural priority within customers' requirements. They only say everything is top priority because they feel compelled to **hoard**.

*"There must be a way to get our customers to prioritize. If I can do that, then I can break the vicious circle."*

### Chapter 32: Small Batches = Small Projects

*"How disappointingly dull. I'd expected thunderclaps and awe, or maybe a Eureka Moment where I jumped out of my office and ran naked down the street."*

*"Small batches were small projects. But that still didn't help me with the prioritization problem."*

Or did it? What if instead of asking customers to prioritize twelve months of work, they asked them to prioritize just the first month?

*"Yes. Small batches would force our customers to prioritize."*
